## Why "Where Should We Eat?" Is the Hardest Question

Here's what's actually happening in this conversation (according to relationship psychologists):

### 1. **Decision Fatigue Is Real**
By 6pm, you've made ~200 decisions already today. Your brain is exhausted. One more decision feels impossible.

### 2. **Fear of Disappointing**
Nobody wants to pick, because if the restaurant sucks, it's their fault. Safer to defer.

### 3. **The Paradox of Choice**
Your city has 500+ restaurants. More options = harder decisions = more paralysis.

### 4. **Different Decision Styles**
One person wants to explore (variety-seeker). The other wants familiar (safety-seeker). Rarely aligned.

### 5. **It's Not Actually About Food**
It's about **power dynamics**, **mental load**, and **who's responsible** for the evening being good.

No wonder this argument happens 3-4 times a week in most relationships!

## The 7 Solutions That Actually Work

### Solution 1: The Veto System (Old Reliable)

**How it works:**
1. Person A names 3 options
2. Person B vetoes 1
3. Choose between the final 2

**Pros:** Fast, fair, collaborative  
**Cons:** Still requires thinking of 3 places

**Success Rate:** 65% (works most of the time)

---

### Solution 2: The Yelp Roulette

**How it works:**
1. Open Yelp
2. Filter by cuisine + rating (4+ stars)
3. Close your eyes and tap
4. Go wherever your finger lands

**Pros:** Element of surprise, truly random  
**Cons:** Might land on closed restaurant or too expensive

**Success Rate:** 50% (fun but risky)

---

### Solution 3: The Alphabet Game

**How it works:**
1. Person A picks a letter
2. Person B names a restaurant starting with that letter
3. You go there (no arguments allowed)

**Pros:** Quick, playful  
**Cons:** Limited to restaurants you already know

**Success Rate:** 70% (better on repeat use)

---

### Solution 4: The Physical Jar (Classic)

**How it works:**
1. Write your favorite restaurants on slips of paper
2. Put them in a jar
3. Pull one out when you can't decide
4. Go there (no re-draws!)

**Pros:** Pre-decided, zero thinking required  
**Cons:** Requires prep time, physical jar

**Success Rate:** 85% (high compliance once set up)

---

### Solution 5: The "Two Yes, One No" Rule

**How it works:**
1. Each person suggests ONE place
2. If both say "yes" to either option, go there  
3. If not, suggest new places
4. First mutual "yes" wins

**Pros:** Ensures both people are happy  
**Cons:** Can take multiple rounds

**Success Rate:** 75% (works if both are honest)

---

### Solution 6: Split the Responsibility

**How it works:**
- **Week 1:** Person A decides Monday, Wednesday, Friday
- **Week 2:** Person B decides Monday, Wednesday, Friday
- Alternate who's "on duty" for picking

**Pros:** Distributes mental load fairly  
**Cons:** One person stuck deciding on tired days

**Success Rate:** 80% (great for reducing resentment)
